# Related Work — Top-Venue Examples (structure-teaching only; do not copy sentences from sources)

> This document teaches **structure and rhetorical moves**, not language. Every description below is a paraphrase of what the section *does*, produced by reading the sources — no sentence is quoted or lifted from any paper. When writing your own Related Work, study the *pattern*, not the *phrasing*.

## 1. GPTQ — ICLR 2023
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2210.17323

- **Structure:** Two clusters, ordered by scale — general post-training quantization methods (originally aimed at smaller vision-scale models) first, then a second cluster of quantization methods specifically attempted on billion-parameter language models. The clusters read as a lineage: classical layer-wise reconstruction techniques → their direct scaled-up descendants.
- **Same/different-axis positioning:** Same-axis: shares the layer-wise, calibration-based reconstruction framework with the first cluster. Different-axis: the paper argues the *scale* at which prior methods remain tractable is orders of magnitude below what's needed for modern LLMs, and frames its contribution as removing that scalability ceiling while keeping accuracy — i.e., differentiates on a quantitative axis (how large a model the method can handle in reasonable time), not on a wholly new mechanism.
- **Citation density:** Moderate, thematically grouped — single citations for naming specific prior methods, multi-citation clusters when asserting "several works in this line."
- **Notable habits:** Uses a capability-gap framing move — first grants that prior work is sound in principle, then pivots on a "but scaling it is hard" turn to motivate the new contribution. The gap is stated in concrete, measurable terms (compute cost, achievable bit-width) rather than as generic criticism.

## 2. PagedAttention / vLLM — SOSP 2023
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.06180

- **Structure:** Three clusters, widening-then-narrowing: general-purpose model-serving systems (broad, not LLM-specific) → transformer/LLM-specific serving systems (narrower, includes the closest prior system) → memory-optimization techniques from adjacent domains (training-time memory tricks, other inference memory schemes). This is a clear lineage-to-competitor funnel: general infrastructure is background, the LLM-serving cluster is where the real competitor sits.
- **Same/different-axis positioning:** Explicitly singles out one prior system as the nearest comparison point and spends the most space on it. Same-axis: both target LLM-serving throughput. Different-axis: the prior system optimizes throughput via request/iteration scheduling, whereas this paper optimizes via memory layout — the paper frames the two as attacking different bottlenecks of the same problem, which lets it claim both superiority *and* potential complementarity rather than pure opposition.
- **Citation density:** Mixed — the survey-style first cluster lists many systems in one grouped sentence; the closest-competitor discussion narrows to single-citation, high-detail treatment.
- **Notable habits:** Names the closest competitor explicitly rather than leaving it buried in a list — a "most relevant to us" style callout — then uses contrast connectives specifically in that one paragraph, reserving sharp contrast language for the true rival while treating the broader cluster more neutrally/descriptively.

## 3. H2O (Heavy-Hitter Oracle) — NeurIPS 2023
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2306.14048

- **Structure:** Four clusters rather than a strict two-part split: general efficient-LLM-inference methods, sparse/low-rank attention approximations, classical caching/eviction theory (borrowed from systems literature, used as conceptual grounding rather than a competitor), and a short cluster explaining the generation-phase mechanics that motivate the problem. Lineage and competition are interleaved rather than cleanly separated into two blocks.
- **Same/different-axis positioning:** Same-axis with the sparse-attention cluster: both reduce effective attention/context cost. Different-axis: prior sparse-attention work still required retaining a large cache footprint or expensive retraining, while this paper targets the cache memory itself using an eviction policy that needs no fine-tuning and is derived from an observed empirical property (a small subset of tokens dominates attention mass). The positioning leans on an "orthogonal angle on an existing problem" framing more than a head-to-head superiority claim.
- **Citation density:** Uneven by design — dense (many grouped citations) in the broad compression/quantization survey cluster, sparse in the eviction-specific cluster, signaling to the reader that this sub-problem is comparatively underexplored.
- **Notable habits:** Uses observation-driven transitions — a "however, X is hard" or "we observe" turn to pivot from surveying prior art into motivating the paper's own empirical premise, rather than a pure logical contrast. Negation-based softer contrasts (prior methods exist but have not seen broad adoption/practicality) are preferred over direct "prior work is worse" claims.

## 4. StreamingLLM — ICLR 2024
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2309.17453

- **Structure:** Three clusters organized by *what property of long-context handling* each line of work targets: length extrapolation (generalizing beyond trained sequence length), context-window extension (making the window itself bigger), and better utilization of long context once available. This is a taxonomy-by-goal rather than by method family.
- **Same/different-axis positioning:** The section makes an explicit point that these three goals are not interchangeable — solving one does not imply solving another — and uses that distinction to place the paper precisely inside the "length extrapolation" cluster while explicitly ruling out overlap with the other two (so readers don't expect this paper to also solve utilization or raw window-size problems). Different-axis claim: prior extrapolation techniques degrade or fail entirely at effectively unbounded length, whereas this paper claims to work indefinitely without fine-tuning.
- **Citation density:** Clustered by technique family, roughly 2–4 citations per named technique, denser around foundational positional-encoding methods.
- **Notable habits:** Strong use of explicit-contrast connectives paired with scope-limiting language ("orthogonal to," "a different axis") to pre-empt the reader's potential objection that a cited method already solves the same problem — the section proactively narrows what it's claiming rather than waiting to be challenged.

## 5. FlashAttention — NeurIPS 2022
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2205.14135

- **Structure:** Pushed into an appendix-level extended discussion (main body keeps only a compressed version), with four clusters: IO-complexity/hardware-aware algorithms (deep lineage, decades old), structured-matrix approaches to efficient computation (theoretical foundation), sparse-training/pruning literature (adjacent but distinct), and efficient/approximate-attention variants (the direct competitor cluster). This is a strong lineage-first, competitors-last ordering — foundational theory is established before any comparison is made.
- **Same/different-axis positioning:** Different-axis pivot is unusually specific: prior efficient-attention work optimized a *theoretical* cost metric (FLOP count), while this paper argues the actually-binding constraint on modern hardware is memory movement, not compute — so it reframes the entire comparison axis rather than just claiming to be faster on the same axis. Same-axis: still computes exact (not approximate) attention, which it also uses to differentiate favorably from the approximate-attention cluster on accuracy grounds.
- **Citation density:** Highly uneven — light in the deep-lineage cluster, very dense (double digits) in the structured-matrices and efficient-transformer clusters, reflecting how crowded that sub-field is.
- **Notable habits:** Prefers implicit/indirect contrast over explicit "unlike X" phrasing — it states what prior approaches overlooked or failed to achieve in practice (theoretically efficient but not translating into real wall-clock gains) rather than directly naming a competitor as inferior. This "acknowledge the theoretical merit, then pivot to a practical shortfall" move recurs across multiple clusters, giving the section a consistent rhetorical signature even though the topics differ.

## 6. SmoothQuant — ICML 2023
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2211.10438

- **Structure:** Three clusters in a strict lineage-to-competitor order: general LLM scaling context (why the problem matters at all), general quantization foundations (spans CNNs and earlier transformer work), and finally LLM-specific quantization competitors. The third cluster is where nearly all the direct positioning happens; the first two are scene-setting.
- **Same/different-axis positioning:** Same-axis: shares the post-training, no-retraining constraint with the closest competitors. Different-axis: the section goes competitor-by-competitor within the third cluster, naming each one's specific failure mode (one incurs runtime overhead from its mixed-precision approach, others degrade or become impractical past a certain model size) before asserting that this method avoids all of those failure modes simultaneously via full-integer, hardware-native quantization. This is a "differentiate against each named rival individually, then claim the union of their strengths without their weaknesses" pattern.
- **Citation density:** Roughly 2–3 citations per paragraph, concentrated in the third (competitor) cluster where each rival gets individual, detailed treatment rather than being bundled.
- **Notable habits:** Heavy, repeated use of contrast connectives each anchored to one specific rival at a time — a one-rival-per-sentence granularity rather than sweeping generalizations about "prior work." Failure modes are stated as capability boundaries ("cannot," "only succeeds up to X"), which makes the differentiation feel evidence-based rather than dismissive.

## 7. KIVI — ICML 2024
**Link:** https://arxiv.org/abs/2402.02750

- **Structure:** Three clusters organized by *what part of the model* prior compression work touches: weight-only quantization, activation-aware/joint weight-activation quantization, and system-level or token-eviction approaches (memory-management systems and heavy-hitter-style eviction policies). Lineage and competition are not sharply separated — most clusters are framed as adjacent rather than rival.
- **Same/different-axis positioning:** Notably softer than the other examples — nearly every cluster is explicitly framed as complementary/orthogonal ("can be combined with," "a different layer of the stack") rather than as something to be beaten. The different-axis claim is precise and narrow: this method needs no fine-tuning, targets a more extreme compression level than the closest same-family competitor, and uses an asymmetric per-channel/per-token quantization split that prior uniform approaches didn't use. The positioning wins on specificity of the differentiator, not on breadth of criticism.
- **Citation density:** Even and moderate (2–3 per paragraph); no single prior work dominates the space, each gets roughly one illustrative sentence.
- **Notable habits:** Consistent "orthogonal/complementary" framing even where the work is arguably a direct competitor — a conflict-minimizing rhetorical choice that still manages to differentiate by being precise about the *mechanism* difference (asymmetric axis choice, tuning-free, extreme bit-width) rather than claiming general superiority.

## Style — constraint words / 写法 (summarized, flexible)

> Derived by generalizing across the 7 examples above — **this is observed pattern, not a fixed checklist**. 参照这些范例归纳出的写法习惯来组织自己的 Related Work，**灵活运用、不逐词照搬、不死板**：具体用哪种过渡词、软/硬对比姿态，取决于你和被引工作的真实关系，不要机械套用下面的词表。

- **Cluster by topic/technique-family or by problem-goal, never by chronology.** Every example groups prior work into 2–4 named clusters (e.g., "weight-only quant" vs "activation-aware quant" vs "system-level"; or "length extrapolation" vs "window extension" vs "utilization"). Pick the axis that matches how your field actually splits (by mechanism, by what problem it solves, by what layer of the stack it touches) — don't force a two-cluster template if your prior work naturally forms three or four groups.
- **Order lineage/foundational clusters before competing/comparison clusters.** Broader, more established background is introduced first to set context; the sharpest, most detailed positioning is reserved for the last cluster — the true rivals. Citation density typically tracks this ordering: denser/grouped citations in the scene-setting clusters, more individualized (one rival, one paragraph) treatment near the end.
- **State the same-axis before the different-axis.** Every example grants shared ground first (same problem, same general constraint, same family of technique) before pivoting to what's different — this reads as more credible than leading with contrast. The pivot is usually a single connective moment (a "however / while / although / in contrast" turn), not scattered throughout.
- **Choose the contrast's rhetorical posture deliberately — sharp vs. soft — based on the real relationship, not by default.** Some examples (SmoothQuant, vLLM's closest-competitor paragraph, StreamingLLM) use direct contrast language and name a specific failure mode of the rival. Others (H2O, KIVI, and FlashAttention's non-competitor clusters) prefer softer "orthogonal to / complementary to / can be combined with" framing when the prior work solves an adjacent rather than identical problem. Using sharp contrast against something that's actually complementary reads as unfair; using soft "orthogonal" framing against a true head-to-head rival reads as evasive — match the posture to the actual overlap.
- **Differentiate on a specific, checkable axis, not a vague quality claim.** Across all seven papers, the "different" side of the same/different pair is stated as a concrete boundary condition — a scale limit, a latency/overhead cost, a need for fine-tuning, a failure past a certain length/size, a theoretical-vs-practical cost metric — rather than "our method is better." This is what lets the differentiation double as the paper's own motivation.
- **Name the closest baseline(s) explicitly, and let that naming anticipate what the experiments section will compare against.** Several examples single out one or two works for extended, specific treatment rather than treating all prior work uniformly — that extended treatment is where the paper stakes its main comparative claim.
- **Citation density is not uniform — let it vary intentionally.** Background/foundational clusters can legitimately group many citations per sentence (survey-style); the cluster containing your closest competitors usually reads better with lower density and one rival discussed per sentence/paragraph, since that's where precision matters most.
- **Section placement/length can flex with venue norms.** Not every example keeps this all in one compact section — one (FlashAttention) compresses the main-body version and pushes extended discussion to an appendix. Match your venue's page budget; the structural moves above hold regardless of how much space you're given.
